Alzheimer's Disease (AD) is a chronic & dreadful neurogenerative disease, the most common form of dementia, and unfortunately, uncurable. Our group's goal is to investigate different diagnostic reports, and predict with significant accuracy which of these are most influential in predicting the stage (or phase) of AD.

Abstract

An Optimum Diagnostic Recipe for Detecting the A.D. Phase


Introduction & Motivation

The what and why?

Alzheimer's Disease (AD) is a neurdegenerative disorder that affects over 5 million people solely within the United States. To add to that, 1 in 3 seniors die with Alzheimer's or another form of dementia. This is such an engrossing topic because this number is expected to increase by 250% in 30 years. The cost of treating is also expected to rise above $200 billion in 2018 alone. For these reasons, we felt it necessary to contribute what we've learned from this class to help reduce on the cost and provide possible suggestions for which diagnostic criteria is critical to study at what stage in the development of the disease.

Machine Learning Methods

The how?

We used a number of different learners in this project. We started off by selecting 12 attributes with the most information gain through decision trees (J48), and then trained (70%) and tested (30%) with neural nets, KNN (n=9), Support Vector Machines, Random Forest Classifiers, and Ada Boost Classifiers. Our results have been tabulated so we could compare which performed best. Our learners achieved varying results per phase in patients, but in general, Random Forest and ADA Boost Classifiers outperformed the others. In addition, of the 12 features, AVDEL30MIN was a strong predictor prior to 6 months of testing, but CDORIENT performed better for all later tests. We describe our steps and results in more detail below.

Data

Step 1

Cleaning

We approached the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) and requested access to their database of diagnosis results from thousands of patients over the years. The database required us to heavily preprocess the data. The database 'scores' each diagnosis based on a Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) and Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) scoring scale. The same is done for the stage of a patient's symptoms Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE). The image to the right represents the various subcategories ADNI has broken up their diagnoses.

Step 2

Selecting favorable attributes

We used a J48 deicision tree learned through Weka to select the attributes that would achieve the highest information gain. We selected the top 12. We found that choosing more than 12 yielded very similar results. As time passes, the number of important attributes we found decreased, suggesting the more common tests may not be strong indicators of the later states of the disease. In particular, the feature diagnosis AVDEL30MIN appeared in the top predictors until 6 months, but the feature diagnosis CDORIENT appeared in all subsequent 6-month phases. Models

Step 3

Testing & Comparing Accuracies

We split the data from each month into 70% training sets and 30% testing sets, and compared all accuracies with and without feature selection. After applying various machine learning models from SciKit-Learn and TensorFlow, we found that the 12 selected attributes achieved a higher accuracy in predicting the disease in the early and late phases of AD, when compared to learning on all 63 attributes. The middle phases of the tests (such as the 2nd year mark) don't produce very high results for either the 12 selected or 63 total attributes. From the comparison, we saw that the selected attributes achieved a higher or comparable accuracy in predicting the disease. We used Neural Nets for the classification task with various hyperparameters to achieve maximum accuracy. For KNN, various values of k was check ranging from 2 to 25 and 9 was found to perform the best in terms of performance and accuracy. Another algorithm that performed well consistently across various phases was Random Forest Classifier with a depth of 7 proving the efficacy of ensemble methods. SVC and Decision Trees didn’t perform consistently with the selected attributes, possibly because of limited data as the number of number increased. Also it was noticed that in the early and later months, such as M06 and M48, the selected attributes performed better than the full entries. In the middle months, like M18, the selected attributes don’t meet our expectation. However, we notice that even the full attributes of this month don’t give high accuracy. It indicates that the diagnosis recipe currently in use for these stages may not be working ideally. Results & Conclusive Remarks

· After testing over 800 models through brute-force hyper-parameterization, we were able to produce very promising results.

· Our accuracies lingered over 80%, therefore there wasn't any clear answer to whether or not isolating the models the 12 specific tests was beneficial.

· We believe that the path to a distinct solution extrapolates from some combination of the models, depending on the phase in question.

· While we didn't have the time to run a Convolutional Neural Net on the MRI Imaging Data, we would like to take on this task in the near future.
